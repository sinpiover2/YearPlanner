Forecast Timeline Vision

Purpose

This document describes the long-term vision for the Forecast Timeline.

It is intentionally aspirational.

Unlike the architecture documents, this document is not constrained by the current implementation.

It describes the experience we are trying to create.

Whenever implementation decisions become difficult, this document should serve as the north star.

?

Imagine

It is Tuesday morning.

A teacher has ten minutes before students arrive.

They open Year Planner.

They are not looking for statistics.

They are not looking for reports.

They are asking one question:

Am I OK?

The Forecast page answers before they consciously finish asking.

Not because it says ﾒYouﾕre OK.ﾓ

Because the page simply looks understandable.

Nothing feels urgent unless it truly is.

Nothing requires decoding.

Nothing competes for attention.

The interface feels calm because the year is calm.

?

The Five-Second Test

Within five seconds, a teacher should understand:

* where today falls within the school year,
* where each course currently stands,
* whether any section needs attention,
* whether required curriculum still fits,
* and whether today requires any action.

Without reading paragraphs.

Without opening menus.

Without interpreting charts.

?

The Timeline Is the Map

The Forecast Timeline is the map of the school year.

Maps exist for orientation.

Not instruction.

The timeline quietly answers:

Where am I?

Everything else builds from that answer.

Teachers should immediately recognize:

* todayﾕs position,
* expected progress,
* current progress,
* projected progress,
* and remaining runway.

The map should make the state of the year obvious.

?

Geometry Tells the Story

The strongest visual language is geometry.

Teachers should understand pacing from:

* position,
* distance,
* alignment,
* movement,
* and projection.

Color reinforces meaning.

Words confirm meaning.

Geometry communicates meaning.

?

Reading Is Optional

The best Forecast page can be understood almost entirely without reading.

A teacher should be able to glance at the page and immediately recognize:

* everything is fine,
* something deserves watching,
* or intervention is required.

The cards exist to explain.

The geometry exists to communicate.

?

The Timeline Never Lies

The timeline represents reality.

If a section is behind, it should appear behind.

If required curriculum no longer fits within the school year, the geometry should reveal that naturally.

Nothing should be hidden for visual convenience.

Calm comes from honesty.

Not simplification.

?

The School Year Is Shared

Every section teaches within the same school year.

The calendar belongs to the year.

The curriculum belongs to the course.

The progress belongs to the section.

Future versions should increasingly separate those ideas visually.

Shared structures should be drawn once.

Differences should stand out naturally.

?

The Forecast Is Visible

Teachers should not have to imagine the future.

The Forecast Timeline should gradually evolve toward showing projected outcomes directly.

Projected movement should become part of the geometry.

The future should feel like a continuation of the present rather than a sentence describing it.

?

Color Is Rare

Most of the page should be quiet.

Neutral.

Unassuming.

When color appears, it should immediately communicate significance.

Teachers should eventually trust color without needing to think about it.

?

The Cards Become Coaches

The timeline answers:

Where am I?

The cards answer:

Why?

What happens next?

What should I do?

The cards should never duplicate the timeline.

They should build upon it.

?

Every Layer Has One Job

The page succeeds because every component has a single responsibility.

Banner:

Am I OK?

Timeline:

Where am I?

Cards:

Why?

Recommendations:

What should I do?

No layer should repeat another.

?

Less Reading Every Year

Each version of Year Planner should require less reading than the previous one.

Not because information is removed.

Because more understanding comes from the visual design itself.

Progress is measured by reducing explanation rather than increasing it.

?

Calm Through Confidence

Teachers already manage uncertainty every day.

The software should not add to it.

Forecasts should acknowledge uncertainty honestly.

The interface should remain calm without pretending everything is perfect.

Confidence comes from truthful representation, not optimistic language.

?

A teacher opens the Forecast page.

Their eyes move naturally across the timeline.

Within moments they understand:

ﾒIﾕm on pace.ﾓ

or

ﾒI should keep an eye on Period 5.ﾓ

or

ﾒI need to make a decision about Period 6.ﾓ

Nothing feels surprising.

Nothing feels confusing.

Nothing feels overwhelming.

The software quietly provides understanding.

The teacher provides judgment.

?

Long-Term Direction

Future versions of the Forecast Timeline will likely:

* communicate more through geometry,
* require less reading,
* reduce duplicated information,
* make future consequences increasingly visible,
* simplify rather than expand,
* and continue separating orientation from interpretation.

Every iteration should move toward greater clarity, not greater complexity.

?

Success

The Forecast Timeline succeeds when teachers stop thinking about the interface.

They simply understand their year.

The interface disappears.

Only understanding remains.
